It walks you through exactly what to do, what actually matters for a Tesla repair, and what isn't always explained clearly when you call your insurance company — so you can make the right decision before anything gets set in motion.

1. What to Do Once You're Safe

Once everyone is out of harm's way and emergency services are contacted if needed — these are the steps that actually determine how your claim and repair play out. This is where most Tesla owners lose ground without realizing it.

  1. 1
    Document everything before anything moves
    Photograph all vehicles from multiple angles, all visible damage, the surrounding area, street signs, skid marks, and traffic signals. Do a full video walk-around. Do this before vehicles are moved if possible. Comprehensive documentation consistently produces better claim outcomes — and protects you if the other party's account changes later.
  2. 2
    Exchange information completely
    Name, license number, insurance company, policy number, vehicle make, model, and plate. Get witness contact information if available. Photograph the other driver's insurance card and license directly — do not rely on memory or handwritten notes.
  3. 3
    Note your Tesla's current state
    Record any warning messages on the touchscreen and alerts in the Tesla app. Note whether airbags deployed or any safety systems activated. A certified shop will ask for this — it informs the repair assessment before teardown begins.
  4. 4
    Do not authorize a tow to any shop yet
    If a tow is needed, you can have the vehicle taken to a secure holding location while you identify the right certified shop. The moment a tow is directed to a specific facility, the repair path begins. You do not have to decide where your Tesla goes in the first hour — and you should not.

High-voltage note: If airbags deployed or the high-voltage system may have been compromised, do not attempt to charge the vehicle or use electrical features until assessed by a qualified technician. Tesla's emergency response guide is available through the Tesla app.

⚠️ Tesla Owners: Save Your Footage Before Doing Anything Else

Tesla vehicles do not automatically save crash footage unless it is manually triggered — and that footage can be overwritten quickly if you continue driving.

  • Tap the Dashcam icon immediately after stopping and select Save Recent Clip
  • If enabled, honk your horn to trigger a recording save without touching the screen
  • Do not continue driving until you confirm the footage is saved in Controls → Safety → Dashcam Viewer
⚠ Critical — no cloud backup exists

If the footage is not saved immediately, it may be permanently lost. Tesla does not store dashcam footage in the cloud. Many drivers assume the vehicle handled this automatically — it did not unless a USB drive was installed and the clip was actively saved.

This footage can determine liability and protect you during the insurance process. It takes seconds to save. It cannot be recovered after the fact.

2. Before You Call Insurance — Read This First

Once a shop is suggested, assigned, or a tow is directed, the repair process begins moving in a specific direction — and changing that direction later becomes more difficult than most people expect.

Most people's first instinct after an accident is to call their insurance company. That's a reasonable response — but there's something important to understand about what happens next.

When you report a Tesla collision to your insurer, they will typically recommend one or more shops from their Direct Repair Program (DRP) network. These are shops that have a pre-existing agreement with the insurance company to handle claims directly. Your insurer may explain that repairs through their network come with a warranty and that the process will be smoother.

That's often true. But what's less commonly explained is how repair standards can differ depending on the shop and the type of vehicle — particularly for a Tesla.

⚠ What to know before you're in this situation

DRP shops are selected based on cost efficiency and cycle time — how cheaply and quickly they complete repairs. Tesla certification is not a requirement for DRP enrollment. A shop can be in your insurer's preferred network without meeting Tesla's manufacturer repair standards.

This doesn't mean every DRP shop does poor work. Many do excellent repairs. But for a vehicle as technically complex as a Tesla — with its aluminum structure, advanced safety systems, and software integration — the certification question is worth asking before you commit to a shop.

For a standard vehicle, this may not create a noticeable difference. For a Tesla, it can.

The goal is not to follow the easiest path — it is to choose the right repair path before it is chosen for you.

The good news: you have options, and California law protects your right to exercise them.

3. Why Tesla Repairs Are Different

A Tesla collision isn't handled the same way as a conventional vehicle repair — and it shouldn't be. Here's what makes Tesla repairs uniquely demanding.

  • Bonded, riveted, and giga-cast structure: A shop that has not completed Tesla's certified program has no access to these repair procedures, no approved tooling, and no access to the OEM parts required to restore the structure correctly. Tesla vehicles are not built like conventional cars. The body combines stamped aluminum panels, high-strength steel sections, extruded components, and large single-piece aluminum giga castings — all joined through a combination of self-piercing rivets, structural adhesives, weld-bonding, and GMA welding. Tesla's own repair guidelines define a structural component as any bonded, welded, weld-bonded, rivet-bonded, or riveted part of the vehicle. Each joint type requires a different repair method, different approved tooling, and specific fasteners sourced directly from Tesla. Frame straightening equipment is prohibited on structural components — pulling or pushing a bonded or rivet-bonded structure can damage joints and surrounding components in ways that are invisible from the outside. The giga castings — large single-piece aluminum sections that replace what were previously 70 or more individual stamped parts — can only be repaired as specifically permitted in Tesla's Collision Repair Manual, using Tesla-approved structural adhesives and fasteners. Non-approved fasteners, even ones that appear identical to OEM parts, cannot be verified to meet Tesla's specifications and are explicitly prohibited.
  • Camera and safety system calibration: Tesla's Autopilot cameras and Active Safety features — including Automatic Emergency Braking and Lane Departure Avoidance — must be recalibrated after any structural or panel work. Tesla's cameras self-calibrate through a specific drive cycle, but a certified shop must first clear calibration data and verify camera pitch. Skipping this leaves safety systems functioning incorrectly with no visible warning.
  • High-voltage system: Tesla's battery and drive systems operate at high voltage and require certified safety protocols during any repair that involves disassembly near the battery or drivetrain.
  • Software integration: Tesla vehicles are deeply software-integrated. Post-repair diagnostics, system resets, and software verification are part of a proper repair process — not optional add-ons.
  • OEM parts requirement: Genuine Tesla parts are only available to shops enrolled in Tesla's Body Repair Program. Non-certified shops use aftermarket alternatives that were not designed, tested, or crash-rated for your specific vehicle.

These aren't minor technical details — they directly affect the safety, reliability, and resale value of your vehicle after a collision repair.

5. Can Insurance Force You to Use Their Shop in California?

✓ California Law — Your Right to Choose

No. In California, your insurance company cannot require you to use a specific repair facility. Under California Insurance Code, you have the legal right to choose where your vehicle is repaired. Your insurer can recommend shops — but recommendation is not the same as requirement.

This is a meaningful protection that many California drivers don't know they have. If your insurer recommends a shop that is not Tesla certified, you can decline that recommendation and request a certified facility instead.

How to use this right effectively:

  • Know the name of a Tesla-certified shop before you call to file your claim
  • When reporting the accident, state the name of the shop you want to use
  • If your insurer pushes back, reference your right under California law to choose your own repair facility
  • Document all communications with your insurer during this process

The key is having a verified shop name ready before you call. Once you're in the claims process and a DRP shop has been assigned, redirecting becomes more complicated — not impossible, but harder.

6. How to Choose the Right Shop for a Tesla Repair in LA

When evaluating a collision shop for your Tesla, these are the factors that actually matter.

  • Current Tesla Body Repair Program enrollment: Ask directly — are they currently enrolled in Tesla's official Body Repair Program? Certification can lapse. Current enrollment is what matters.
  • Dedicated aluminum repair environment: A proper Tesla repair requires a separate, contamination-controlled aluminum repair area. This is a facility requirement, not optional.
  • Camera calibration process: Confirm the shop clears calibration data, verifies camera pitch, and initiates recalibration as part of their standard Tesla repair — not as an optional add-on.
  • Genuine Tesla OEM parts only: Ask specifically about their parts policy. Certified shops use only genuine Tesla OEM parts. Any mention of "equivalent" or "aftermarket" parts is a signal to look elsewhere.
  • Insurance handling experience: A shop that works regularly with insurance companies will know how to navigate the approval process. Ask how many Tesla claims they handle per month — volume indicates real experience.
  • Repair documentation: A quality shop will document their process and provide a post-repair summary. This matters for warranty protection and when you eventually sell the vehicle.

7. Los Angeles-Specific Considerations

Repairing a Tesla in Los Angeles comes with some practical realities that are specific to this market.

  • High Tesla density creates shop demand: Los Angeles has one of the highest concentrations of Tesla vehicles in the United States. Certified shops in LA often have significant backlogs — especially after weather events or on high-accident corridors like the 405, 110, and 10 freeways. Wait times at certified shops can run 2–6 weeks for significant repairs.
  • Parts availability: Tesla parts are distributed through Tesla's own supply chain. In a high-volume market like LA, parts for common repair scenarios are generally available, but specialty components can take time. Your shop's relationship with Tesla's parts program affects lead times.
  • Insurance adjuster familiarity: LA adjusters handle a high volume of Tesla claims. The more experienced adjusters understand Tesla's repair requirements — but not all do. A certified shop with strong insurer relationships can advocate for correct repair procedures on your behalf.
  • Rental coverage gaps: LA's geography makes transportation without a vehicle genuinely difficult. Confirm your rental coverage limits before your repair begins — Tesla repairs often take longer than standard vehicles, and rental coverage gaps become expensive quickly.
  • Freeway accident complexity: High-speed freeway collisions often involve more extensive structural damage than surface street accidents. On an aluminum vehicle like a Tesla, structural damage assessment requires proper teardown — costs and timelines established from exterior inspection alone are often inaccurate.

8. What Happens Next — The Repair Process Overview

Understanding the repair process helps you set realistic expectations and ask the right questions at each stage.

  1. 1
    Initial estimate
    The shop writes an initial estimate based on visible damage. For Tesla repairs, this is rarely the final number — hidden structural damage is common and only visible after teardown.
  2. 2
    Teardown and damage assessment
    Once the vehicle is disassembled, the full scope of damage becomes visible. The shop submits a revised estimate to your insurance company. This is the most critical phase — a thorough teardown by a certified shop protects you from incomplete repairs.
  3. 3
    Insurance approval
    Your insurer reviews and approves the revised estimate. Disputes between the shop and insurer about repair procedures or parts — if any — happen here. A certified shop with established insurer relationships navigates this more effectively.
  4. 4
    Parts ordering
    Genuine Tesla OEM parts are ordered through Tesla's supply chain. Lead times vary by part — plan for 1–3 weeks on average for structural components in the LA market.
  5. 5
    Structural and body repair
    Structural repairs are completed first, followed by body panel work and paint. Tesla's aluminum structure requires specific repair sequences that differ from conventional steel vehicle repair.
  6. 6
    Camera calibration and systems verification
    Calibration data is cleared, camera pitch is verified, and the recalibration process is initiated. All Active Safety features are confirmed functional before delivery. This is required — not optional.
  7. 7
    Final inspection and delivery
    The vehicle is inspected, cleaned, and returned. A quality shop will walk you through what was repaired, what parts were used, and provide documentation of the work performed.

Total timeline in LA: A significant Tesla collision repair in Los Angeles typically runs 4–8 weeks from drop-off to delivery when accounting for teardown, insurance approval, parts ordering, and repair time. Plan accordingly — and confirm your rental coverage covers this window.
